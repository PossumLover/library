We're all perverts now

October 30th, 2022: Rishi

The United Kingdom has now had three female Prime Ministers, and two from ethnic minority backgrounds,1 and all of them have been Tories. Rishi Sunak is now our first non-white PM. How should we feel about this? The ordinary people they interview on TV keep insisting that they don’t remotely care, but the TV keeps asking them; clearly there’s someone who cares, or someone who cares about whether or not anyone cares. A few people in India seem happy; Americans are being predictably weird. The official stance of the Labour Party is that this is a great and wonderful thing, to the point that a Labour MP (also of Indian origin) was forced to delete a tweet in which she suggested that Sunak’s Punjabi granny might be less important than his actual intentions with the country. It is nice to live in a democracy.

You should know the drill by now. As actual governance is outsourced to financial institutions, political discourse abandons the question of what politicians can do for you, and starts revolving around the question of what they are. Americans are particularly good at this stuff. Hillary Clinton was, famously, a woman. She might have also had some other political and human qualities, but any discussion of those was widely held to be cruel and bigoted, and motivated entirely by the only salient fact about her, which is that she is a woman. Barack Obama was intermittently black, when it suited him, but mostly he was young. Being black suits Kamala Harris better; Donald Trump was consistently acknowledged as white.

In Britain we do things slightly differently. The Conservatives are smart about this: they drop promising ethnic-minority candidates into safe white rural seats, usher them into the Cabinet, and never, ever mention what they’re doing. But Labour are stupid; they talk quotas. Maybe like the Americans, they haven’t noticed just how conservative this personality-politics really is. In the UK, we’ve already shattered that highest, hardest glass ceiling, and it was Margaret Thatcher wot did it. But you still get scenes like Yvette Cooper’s in 2015. ‘Who’s the real radical? Jeremy Corbyn? Or me, a woman?’

The usual name for this complex is identitarianism or identity politics, but I think it’s really just a facet of something much bigger. Call it political perversion. Freud, in his Three Essays, describes sexual perversion as any deviation from the straightforward heterosexual aim, which is cumming inside; political perversion is any deviation from the straightforward aim of politics, which is the reorganisation of society along your preferred lines. The political pervert keeps taking his eyes off the prize: he gets lost in signals and symbols and fetishes. Politics becomes a secret pleasure, or a kind of adornment for the self: I believe this, I support this, because I’m a good person. Protesters want to be looked at more than they want any particular change. Elected officials take office to promote their Instagram accounts. Referenda are won to send a message. You vote for whoever will make your ideological enemies most upset. Nothing ever happens, and everything means too much.

When critics notice this, they usually start encouraging us to drop the bullshit and get down to real politics. Remember what’s at stake! But maybe it’s not so easy. Freud writes like a baffled alien, blankly observing he madness of human affairs. Even in the most straight-laced circles, perversion reigns. ‘The kiss is held in high sexual esteem among many nations (including the most highly civilised ones), in spite of the fact that the parts of the body involved do not form part of the sexual apparatus but constitute the entrance to the digestive tract.’ A genuinely non-perverse sexual relation is inconceivable; without some kind of perversion we’re just joylessly slapping bodies against each other. And the people who do choose to forgo the kiss, or any meaningful relation to the sexual object—they’re perverts too: they don’t get off on the mechanical act of fucking a total stranger, but the symbolic act of fucking a total stranger. Sex itself is not that interesting. We start out polymorphously perverse; every orthopraxy must float on a dark sea of deviation. Politics works the same way. I am not a particularly political person, and I’m not very good at writing about politics, but there’s no such thing as politics, not any more. I am a pervert. I am lost in the spectacle of it all, and so are you.

One vast rolling field, rolling over everything, where lambs dance in the final frosts of springtime and fill the supermarkets by Easter! One of everything! One!

October 14th, 2022: Vince

Across Europe, climate change protesters have started defacing works of art: they threw soup at a Van Gogh, smeared mashed potato over a Monet, and glued themselves to a Vermeer. ‘What is worth more: art or life? Are you more concerned about the protection of a painting or the protection of our planet and people?’ After all, if our planet really is doomed, then all the art goes with it.2 Why not start now? So the protesters set up a contradiction between art and life, and tell us that they are on the side of life. Fine—we’ve had iconoclasts before; it’s an ethos. But as an act of effective protest, this is almost entirely useless.

Even if art is less important than life, attacking art doesn’t necessarily make life any more viable. It’s an awareness-raising spectacle: make lots of noise, get your pictures in the paper, and people might start to feel that they really are in crisis… Doesn’t everyone already know? How much more awareness needs to be raised before that awareness starts transforming into results? I’m aware, but I still need to heat my home like everyone else. There is a form of climate activism that can effectively produce its desired results, and it’s the Andreas Malm route. A stochastic campaign of sabotage and terrorism against fossil-fuel infrastructure, using homemade explosives, on a scale sufficient to manipulate the global energy markets and artificially reduce the relative cost of renewable options. But this year’s spike in oil prices has simulated what this might look like, and it looks like everyone getting poorer, except shareholders in energy companies. This would not make the activists particularly popular, so they’ve decided to not be activists at all.

The art vandalisms don’t make much sense as protest, but they do make sense as art. A performance, a theatre, an artwork commenting—like all contemporary art—on the fetishistic value of art itself. It is a little derivative; artists have been destroying each other’s works for some time now. There’s Robert Rauschenberg’s Erased de Kooing Drawing, Ai Weiwei’s Dropping a Han Dynasty Urn, Jake and Dinos Chapman defacing Goya’s etchings, and Yuan Chai and Jian Jun Xi jumping on Tracey Emin’s bed. It might have been better, more real, if these people had actually ruined something instead of only picking out those paintings protected behind glass. But the climate angle is nice, and its pedigree is sound; the Just Stop Oil protests are a perfectly legitimate work of art. Except—which is worth more? Art or life?

July 7th, 2022: Boris

We got rid of Boris Johnson because he was a bad person. And it’s true; he really is a bad person. If you were the type that likes to dole out diagnoses from a distance, you could probably call Boris a psychopath: very superficially charming, with all his fat-cheeked bluster, his posho pleonasm, that carefully contrived character he plays—but there’s something icy underneath, something lizardy and cruel. This is a man who believes in nothing except himself, and cares about nothing except power. In a previous age he might have been like his ancestors in the Ottoman aristocracy, slithering his way to the Sublime Porte on a path greased with blood. A dagger in his brother’s back, a harem full of murdered women and eunuchs, a drop of poison in the Sultan’s wine… But he lived in Britain in the twenty-first century, so his tools were different. For most of his life, he pretended to be an amiable, slightly scatterbrained upper-class buffoon. He had a particular talent for falling off bikes and into nearby bodies of water, crawling out of the pond slime with a wonky grin.

But every so often the mask would slip. In 2015, for instance, when he was on a junket to Japan as Mayor of London, invited to play a friendly game of rugby against some local schoolchildren. Someone passed him the ball; a touchdown was within reach, with just one skinny ten-year-old defender in the way… Johnson could have easily nipped around the child, but he didn’t; he threw him to the ground with one heave of his considerable Mayoral bulk. For a moment, he must have forgotten where he was and why he was there, forgotten that it’s not appropriate for elected officials to rugby-tackle a small child. The hunger took over, that old restless urge of the British ruling classes to dominate and destroy. The hordes of Asia break against his might! The Sun will never set again!

The longer Boris was in power, the lower that mask fell. You remember the scandals. How he was holding boozy parties while the law forbade everyone else from burying their parents. How he wouldn’t even say how many children he’d had with how many women. How one of his political allies had taken money from private companies and then illegally lobbied for their interests in Parliament, and how Boris had responded by simply trying to dissolve the Parliamentary Standards Committee. ‘Letter boxes.’ Lies. The thing about these scandals, though, is that none of them were particularly political; none of them had any effect on the way government interacts with the lives of its citizenry. They were pure perversion: we’re still in the vortex of his personality, his squalid magnetism. Nobody is thinking politically.

And in the end, when his own party turned on him and Boris fell, the TV and the newspapers had to pretend with a straight face that the entire political and economic direction of the country was about to change over the question of a grabbed arse.

All the nice liberal perverts were celebrating. Why? Did you think they’d let you have Keir Starmer? Did you forget that our next Prime Minister would be chosen by 170,000 of the country’s most retired colonels? Boris Johnson only cared about himself, which meant he was never particularly attached to the pieties of his own party, or even to the class project of British Conservatism. With Corbyn gone, he was the only major figure in British politics not wedded to the logic of austerity. Maybe this makes me a bad leftist, but I think that if you choose to care more about what government actually does than the moral character of the person leading it, this is important. Instead of depressing wages as an enticement to capital, which had long been unofficial Conservative policy, Boris used state investment to prop them up. Instead of starving the working classes into submission, he bought them off. He turned an instinctive Thatcherite like Rishi Sunak into the face of his new brand of Tory Keynesianism. Extraordinary promises: £100 billion for infrastructure, £36 billion for the NHS, even £150 million to support community ownership of cultural institutions. Remember the summer of 2020? Remember Eat Out To Help Out? I do. The great weeknight feeding frenzy, descending on Soho for £10 off fancy broths and molluscs, the streets hot and thronged and perfumed, everyone dressed up sexy after a long lockdown alone, and all of it subsidised by your taxes… I was born under Thatcher; this was the first time any government policy ever directly improved my life.

Boris was a nihilist. And the people competing to replace him were mostly nihilists too; they just lack his imagination. They settle for the package of Default Governance in the twenty-first century, austerity plus antiwokeness. The faction that’s taken over the Labour party are the same: they can only promise that when they impose austerity, they’ll feel bad about it. Look what you did! We had something, not the best we could hope for, but something, and you ruined it! You maniacs! You sick perverted freaks!
